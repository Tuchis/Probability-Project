Permutations are simply possible arrangement of your group of objects. For instance, all possible lines consisting of 30 students of group staying in a queue to Trapesna.

Allocations are somewhat similar to permutations, but now you can choose how many objects out of group you want to arrange(arrangement of a subset of objects, which are interesting for our event). Choosing how to order students of class, such that first three will be the president of class and vice president is an example of such allocation.

Combinations involve, generally speaking, picking a subset out of a larger set. Choosing three students from a class of 26 would be an example of combinations, or picking out three blueberries out of a bush with 600 berries. With combinations, order doesn’t matter.

Combinations with Repetition. Assume that we have a set A with n elements. Any selection of r objects from A, where each object can be selected more than once, is called a combination of n objects taken r at a time with repetition. For instance, the combinations of the letters a, b, c, d taken 3 at a time with repetition are: aaa, aab, aac, aad, abb, abc, abd, acc, acd, add, bbb, bbc, bbd, bcc, bcd, bdd, ccc, ccd, cdd, ddd. Two combinations with repetition are considered identical if they have the same elements repeated the same number of times, regardless of their order. ${C}_{n+k-1}^{k-1} = rac{(n+k-1)!}{n!(k-1)!}$
